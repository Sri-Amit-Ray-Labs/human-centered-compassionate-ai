# Principles of the Sri Amit Ray Compassionate AI Architecture™ (CAIA™)

## Purpose

The Sri Amit Ray Compassionate AI Architecture™ (CAIA™) is founded on the principle that artificial intelligence should serve humanity by advancing human well-being, reducing suffering, supporting ethical decision-making, and promoting sustainable societal progress.

Every algorithm in the Human-Centered Compassionate AI (HC-CAI) Algorithm Series — the practical implementation layer of CAIA™ — is built against the same five-principle ethical charter. Each algorithm's own "Ethical and Design Principles" (or, for the three pre-banner algorithms, "Ethical Dimension") section restates these five ideas in language specific to what that algorithm does. This document consolidates that charter, under the CAIA™ name, and shows how each algorithm applies it. Nothing below is inferred or invented — every quote and claim traces to a specific algorithm's own document in [`docs/algorithms/`](../algorithms/).

## The Five Principles

### 1. Non-harm (Ahimsa)

The foundational constraint: an algorithm's outputs must never create or amplify harm, even as a side effect of optimizing something else. Several algorithms go further and describe themselves as direct enforcement mechanisms for this principle — Algorithm 16 (Ahimsa Decision) states it "*is* the non-harm mechanism; it exists to enforce the principle, not merely reference it," and Algorithm 18 (Harm Prevention) describes itself as "the real-time enforcement layer for non-harm, continuously, not just at decision time." At civilizational scale, Algorithm 25 frames steering away from collapse basins as "the largest-scale expression of non-harm in the framework."

**Evaluation question:** Does the system avoid creating or amplifying harm, including as a side effect of its primary objective?

### 2. Human Dignity

Recommendations and interventions must respect autonomy, privacy, and the inherent worth of the person — never reduce people to a score, a profile, or a means to another end. This ranges from Algorithm 1's caution that "vulnerability weighting exists to protect dignity, not to profile people for other purposes," to Algorithm 24's civilizational claim that "near-zero discounting affirms that future human lives carry the same moral weight as present ones."

**Evaluation question:** Does the system treat every person as an end in themselves, never merely as an optimization target?

### 3. Fairness and Inclusion

Outcomes, error rates, and benefits must be audited across demographic, linguistic, and socioeconomic groups rather than assumed to be neutral. Algorithm 12 (Fairness and Bias Mitigation) makes this its central purpose, requiring that "the chosen fairness criterion and its trade-offs are documented and publicly reported." Other algorithms apply the same audit discipline to their own domain — Algorithm 3 audits "recognition accuracy... per demographic and linguistic group"; Algorithm 21 checks that "deferral and scheduling decisions are checked for unjustified impact on under-resourced regions or teams."

**Evaluation question:** Does the system produce equitable outcomes across the groups it affects, and are the trade-offs disclosed?

### 4. Human Oversight

High-stakes, ambiguous, or irreversible decisions stay with a human rather than resolving automatically. Algorithm 15 (Human Oversight Assurance) describes itself as the oversight mechanism the rest of the architecture depends on, "itself subject to audit for nominal versus meaningful review." Algorithm 25 requires "review by an independent, pluralistic body" for every containment-protocol activation and is "strictly advisory." Algorithm 2 and others escalate infeasible or high-distress cases to a human rather than relaxing protective floors.

**Evaluation question:** Does a human retain genuine, meaningful authority over consequential decisions — not a rubber-stamp role?

### 5. Transparency

Scores, weights, thresholds, and rationale are logged and auditable rather than hidden inside a black box. Algorithm 14 (Transparency and Explainability) notes that it "*is* the transparency mechanism; its own outputs are subject to the same faithfulness standard it imposes on others." Algorithm 10 publishes its weighting parameters outright: "`λ` and `β` weights are public and contestable."

**Evaluation question:** Can an affected stakeholder understand, audit, and contest how a decision was reached?

## How Each Algorithm Documents Its Principles

22 of the 25 algorithms (1–3, 7–25) use the full five-item "Ethical and Design Principles" format above, each restated in that algorithm's own domain language — see the individual document's own section for the exact wording. Three algorithms predate this standardized format and use a shorter "Ethical Dimension" section instead:

| Algorithm | Format | Items |
|---|---|---|
| [04 — PRPA](../algorithms/HC-CAI-Algo-04-PRPA.md) | Ethical Dimension (pre-banner) | Non-harm principle; Transparency; Privacy and dignity |
| [05 — HFOA](../algorithms/HC-CAI-Algo-05.md) | Ethical and Design Principles (pre-template wording) | Non-harm (Ahimsa); Human dignity; Fairness and inclusion; Human oversight; Transparency |
| [06 — RIM](../algorithms/HC-CAI-Algo-06-RIM.md) | Ethical Dimension (pre-banner) | Non-harm; Privacy and consent; Bias mitigation |

Algorithm 5's principles list maps onto the same five ideas as the standard 22, just in its own pre-template wording (e.g., "Strong penalties for harm and addiction, conservative behavior under uncertainty" for non-harm). Algorithms 4 and 6 condense to three items focused on the specific risks of pain-signal and neuro-physiological data — non-harm, transparency, and privacy/consent/bias — rather than the full five-item list. No item has been added to or inferred for these three documents beyond what each already states.

## Governance Commitment

Systems developed under the CAIA™ framework are expected to:

* Advance human well-being and reduce avoidable harm (Non-harm).
* Respect human dignity and autonomy (Human Dignity).
* Promote fairness and inclusion, with disclosed trade-offs (Fairness and Inclusion).
* Maintain meaningful human authority over consequential decisions (Human Oversight).
* Ensure transparency and auditability of scores, weights, and rationale (Transparency).

These five commitments provide the ethical and operational foundation of every algorithm in the HC-CAI series, and the standard against which new algorithms added to the series should be measured. See [Sri-Amit-Ray-CAIA-Governance.md](Sri-Amit-Ray-CAIA-Governance.md) for how this charter connects to external standards (NIST AI RMF, the EU AI Act, ISO/IEC 42001, and IEEE 7000-series standards) and to real-world accountability mechanisms.

## Guiding Statement

> Artificial intelligence should not only become more intelligent. It should become more compassionate, more transparent, more responsible, and more beneficial to humanity.

The Sri Amit Ray Compassionate AI Architecture™ (CAIA™) establishes a framework for advancing human-centered, ethical, explainable, and compassionate artificial intelligence in service of humanity.
